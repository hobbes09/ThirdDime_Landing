One with the Queen and her children

In the third painting everything from the Queen's appearence to the surrounding potrays the Queen as a plain, no fuss lady. The children are central in the attempt to garner empathy.

The first portrait shows the Queen as a fashionista with a bold actressy pose. Her dress, wig, everything about her is magnificent and clearly a testimony of her stature and power. The room has large stage curtains, the Queen has her hand on a globe, a symbol of her dominion on large territories.
The second portrait shows the Queen in a plain English muslin dress with a straw hat. Quite ordinary, one would say and may be an attempt to relate to the general masses. But a Queen who shows herself like this in public is insulting the people and France.
In the third painting the Queen is shown with her children in an austere surrounding. She is dressed in red velvet, authoritative but not extravagant. She is wearing no jewellery, a wise move given her nickname ‘Madame Deficit’. Her portrayal as a mother is also an attempt to garner empathy from the citizenry.
